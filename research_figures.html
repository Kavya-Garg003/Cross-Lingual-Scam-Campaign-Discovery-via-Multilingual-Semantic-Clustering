Figure 2 — System Architecture
Fig. 2. End-to-end system architecture for multilingual scam campaign discovery. Messages in five languages are encoded into a shared semantic space using a multilingual transformer, enabling language-agnostic similarity comparison. UMAP reduces dimensionality in two stages: 50D for clustering and 2D for visualization. HDBSCAN identifies variable-density campaign clusters without requiring a predefined number of groups. Runtime figures measured on NVIDIA T4 GPU (encoding) and CPU (UMAP, clustering) at full 25,411-message scale.

Figure 5 — Baseline Comparison
Silhouette score Clusters found Noise rate (%)
Fig. 5. Comparison of our proposed pipeline against three baselines across three metrics. TF-IDF + DBSCAN achieves the lowest silhouette score (0.374), as bag-of-words representations cannot align cross-lingual semantics. BERTopic, while using the same embedding model, yields 493 fragmented topics with 16.8% noise — over-splitting campaign groups. The English-only SBERT ablation confirms that multilingual embeddings are essential for meaningful cross-lingual clustering. Our method achieves the highest silhouette (0.726) with a low, controlled noise rate of 8.4%.

Why density-based works better
K-Means forces every message into a cluster. Unique scam variants contaminate clean campaign groups.
HDBSCAN identifies 2,143 noise points — these are genuinely rare, one-off messages that don't belong to any campaign, keeping clusters pure.
Variable density support lets HDBSCAN find both large campaigns (1,159 msgs) and small campaigns (30 msgs) in the same run.
Fig. 7. (Left) Proportion of messages assigned to campaign clusters vs. labelled as noise (-1 class) by HDBSCAN. The 8.4% noise rate is intentional: density-based clustering correctly identifies messages that do not fit any recurring pattern. (Right) Qualitative comparison of K-Means and HDBSCAN for scam campaign discovery, justifying the use of a density-based approach.
